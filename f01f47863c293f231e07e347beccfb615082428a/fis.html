Feed Ingest Service (FIS) Product Discovery

THE BACKGROUND

FIS is a backend service that powers the front-end experience of externally produced podcasts on NPR platforms.

In addition to owned and operated podcasts, NPR also distributes podcasts produced by third-parties. As a result, NPR needs a way to view, manage, and troubleshoot these kinds of podcasts and their RSS feeds.

THE TEAM

This team comprised of myself as the only designer, a product manager, two software engineers, a QA engineer, and an engineering manager.

THE METHODOLOGY & TOOLS

Since this was a discovery effort, I made an effort to use research and collaborative workshops as much as possible. I conducted standard user research studies and designed/facilitated custom workshops. I then created workflows and service design diagrams in Figma.

The Problem & Opportunity

There were approximately 3,000 non-NPR podcasts from around 650 local network stations and other third-party publishers that NPR distributes audio content for on NPR platforms like NPR.org and NPR One mobile app. This is accomplished via podcast RSS feed ingestion, which means the publisher creates a RSS feed and provides the link to the RSS feed to NPR for NPR to process and utilize.

Establishing a way of ingesting, storing, and playing these podcasts and episodes was an important effort. In the NPR One app alone, local network station and third-party podcasts account for 1.8 hours and 2.5 hours of listening per user, respectively. Additionally, for local network stations, NPR One accounts for 29% of their podcast downloads, making it a key source of podcast engagement for them.

The tool that previously ingested 3rd-party RSS feeds lived inside NPR's old CMS. Since NPR is currently in the process of sunsetting the old CMS for a newer one, we had an opportunity to take apart the old tool, improve workflows, and expand tool functionality to better serve the needs of the people who need to use it.

Project Definitions

Station podcasts

These podcasts come from local stations throughout the United States. They are stations that are part of the NPR network (e.g. WAMU in Washington, DC; KQED in the Bay Area; WUTC in Chattanooga, TN; KUOW in Seattle, WA; etc.).

There are also podcasts that come from public-media adjacent organizations like America Public Media (APM) and Public Radio Exchange (PRX). For the purpose of this portfolio piece, I am also referring to this type of podcasts as “Station podcasts.”

Third-party podcasts

These podcasts are from independent podcasters either from individuals, groups, or companies. (e.g. 1619 from the New York Times; Today, Explained from Vox Media; Freakonomics Radio from Freakonomics Radio and Stitcher, etc.)

Discovery Problem Statement.

NPR needs a new content metadata ingest process, one with minimal intervention from staff, to power NPR experiences with Member station and third-party podcasts. This new process will ease triage and support and add opportunities for NPR as a distributor, while managing only the relevant podcast metadata.


Existing Plucker Tool Research

With the old tool (aka "Podcast Plucker Tool"), colleagues at NPR either lacked the capabilities of truly managing 3rd-party podcasts and/or spent more time troubleshooting errors than managing podcasts. This was largely because:

Error messages provided very little to no information.

The very technical error messages and lack of functionality precluded those who utilized the tool for podcast curation from attempting to troubleshoot by themselves. In fact, the support team also found the error messaging to be unhelpful because the messages did not provide any context on where the issue was or how to solve it.

The Plucker Tool was a passive one

Someone could see that there was an issue, but no visibility into what the issue was or how to solve it. In order to solve it, the NPR One Editorial team had to submit a support ticket to the OnlineTech team. This inundated the support queue with requests that, in many cases, could have been self-troubleshooted (if the tool provided the capabilities, that is).

Multiple sources of disparate information converges and is displayed in this tool

This tool was was the single point where information from two sources of podcast information converge: local station podcasts and true third-party podcasts. These come from different sources with their own standards, making it difficult for staff to make sense of what they were reading and how different standards affected what was displayed.

Defining User Groups

Throughout the rest of discovery, we referenced these user groups cards to center our work and potential solutions around specific needs and painpoints.

Workflow Diagramming

Because we were able to blue sky the potential solution, I started with workflow diagrams to begin designing happy paths for use cases. I took this opportunity to reimagine how NPR ingests and manages these types of podcasts that we do not own.


Error Handling & Support Escalation

A major update to the status quo, I designed self-troubleshooting for station and third-party podcasters. With about 2,500 podcasts that can potentially be incorrectly ingested, internal NPR teams spent a lot of time responding to requests and issues from external podcasters. With this new design, station and third-party podcasters will gain visibility into their podcasts in NPR systems and self-troubleshoot issues before escalating to someone at NPR.


Core Experiences Mapping

I also outlined workflow pathways for how major actions like adding a podcast to NPR systems would happen, depending on who you are. If you are someone at NPR, you can directly add a podcast to the station/third-party podcast directory and the station/third-party will be able to see it in their account dashboard.

At the same time, if a station or third-party podcaster adds a podcast themselves, the podcast will go to a review stage, where the editorial team at NPR will review and approve or deny the addition of the podcast.

Opening up this, and other similarly common and important, functionality would benefit both external users (i.e. station staff and third-party podcasters) by providing visibility and control over their podcasts and internal users (i.e. NPR One editorial team and digital support staff) by freeing up time for these teams from offloading work to the external user.

Podcast Management Co-creation/Research

To delve deeper into the needs of our main user groups (NPR One editorial team and OnlineTech digital support team), I designed and facilitated light-weight co-creation research sessions. Before this research, we only knew a few basic actions for what it means to manage a podcast. So, we recruited people from the team who curate third-party podcast listening experiences (NPR One editorial team) and from the team who troubleshoots issues with third-party podcasts as they arise (OnlineTech digital support team).

Co-creation Document Setup

I created a Figma file with post-its with potential management actions on the left and a co-creation board with quadrants along the axes of "rarely use" to "frequently use" on the left/right axis and "essential" to "optional" on the top/down axis. Participants would place post-it notes (or create new ones) with actions along this board to categorize them.

Sessions

This is how my desktop looked on my side as a facilitator. I shared the Figma screen with the participant, opened a separate window for our meeting open so I could see them, and kept a window open with the session script/agenda. These co-creation sessions were conducted entirely remotely.

These sessions allowed me to see general importance of specific actions and the difference in categorizations between the NPR One editorial team and the OnlineTech digital support team.

There are two types of management:

1. Manipulation

NPR One editorial team does all the podcast manipulation as they curate listening experiences with station or third-party podcasts.
This includes actions like: adding, deleting, reorganizing, and updating contact information.

2. Troubleshooting

OnlineTech digital support team does all the technical troubleshooting (usually related to the RSS feed).
This includes actions like: reingesting a feed, updating metadata, and pinpointing discrepancies to find issues.

Podcast management takes a lot of time and coordination with people outside the organization.

Most podcast issues are issues that our internal teams are not able to resolve because they are not the owners of the podcast RSS feed. The stations and third-parties are! Thus, podcast management and troubleshooting took up a lot of coordination and communication with people outside the organization, which takes up a lot of time.

End of Discovery

We could have continued with discovery, but at some point we needed to make decisions and start shaping the solution we wanted to implement. So, with all the information we learned during our discovery project, we came up with a solution: create a tool that is both externally and internally facing. Externally, third-party podcasters and member stations would be able to manage and troubleshoot their podcasts in the tool. This would lift the burden off of the people at NPR who interface with third-party podcasters and member station staff. Internally, the tool would provide an admin-level view of all third-party and station podcasts that had been entered into NPR systems.

Find out more about how we built out that solution and about my process of designing the tool in my portfolio piece on the internal version of FIS/Podcast Producers NPR Dashboard (PaNDa).

Reflection

This was the first multi-sprint discovery I had participated in, and I took it as an opportunity to really insert human-centered design principles to provide strategy direction. When we first started this discovery effort, we only had a general problem space. The sky was the limit; this was both daunting and freeing. I had endless opportunities and paths to take, but a limited time to make choices. So, I let research help guide the way. Use research as the starting point for answering questions and centering the people who would be most affected by the end result in the process. Let their experiences and knowledge guide the direction of the project. And if there were more questions (and of course there were), continue asking and building clarity around the problem and path to a solution.
